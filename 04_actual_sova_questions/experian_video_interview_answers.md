## About the Sova Video Interview Format

Experian uses **Sova**, an asynchronous video interview platform, as part of its early-stage interview screening. Key characteristics:

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Format** | Recorded video — no live interviewer |
| **Number of questions** | Typically 4 questions |
| **Response time limit** | Usually 90 seconds per answer |
| **Preparation time** | Brief thinking window before recording starts |
| **Retakes** | Usually limited or unavailable — treat each attempt as final |
| **Evaluation** | AI analysis + human review for competency signals |

### What Sova Is Looking For

Sova's AI analysis and Experian's reviewers are evaluating you on:
- **Clarity and coherence** of communication
- **Specificity** — concrete examples, not vague generalities
- **Behavioral competency signals** — collaboration, innovation, learning, initiative
- **Authentic engagement** — looking at the camera, confident delivery
- **STAR structure** — situation → task → action → result

---

## Sova Interview Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Test your camera, microphone, and lighting **before** the interview
- [ ] Choose a quiet, neutral background — professional setting matters
- [ ] Practice each answer out loud until you can deliver it in ≤ 90 seconds
- [ ] Maintain eye contact with the **camera lens**, not the screen
- [ ] Speak at 80% of your normal conversational speed
- [ ] Start each answer with a clear scene-setter (Situation) within 10 seconds
- [ ] End each answer on the **Result** — never trail off mid-action
- [ ] Have a glass of water nearby
- [ ] Dress professionally from the waist up

## Q1 — Why Did You Apply to This Role?

**Full question:**  
*"Each role at Experian plays a key part in helping us achieve our success. Why did you apply to this role?"*

**Competency tested:** Cultural fit, role-specific motivation, professional self-awareness

---

### 📝 Polished Answer (~85 seconds)

Throughout my Master's in Electronic Engineering, I've been building my ML and AI skills not just to understand how models work, but to apply them to problems that genuinely matter. When I came across this role on the Innovation Engineering team, three things immediately stood out.

First, the technical scope is exactly where I want to grow. The role spans classical supervised learning — gradient boosting, random forests — alongside cutting-edge generative AI work with transformers and diffusion models. That range matches my current research trajectory almost perfectly.

Second, the team's mandate for innovation is what I'm looking for. Graduate research has taught me that the most interesting ML challenges are the ones that don't have established playbooks yet. The Innovation Engineering team at Experian is clearly operating in that space.

Third, Experian's scale and domain create a genuinely unique opportunity. Applying ML to financial data at Experian's scale — with the potential to impact credit access and financial inclusion for real people — is meaningful work, not just technical exercise.

I applied because this role sits at the exact intersection of machine learning research depth, software engineering practice, and real-world impact that I've been working toward. I believe my background — particularly my experience training and evaluating ML models and my recent work with transformer architectures — means I can contribute meaningfully from day one.

---

### 🔑 Key Phrases to Include

- "Innovation Engineering team" — shows role-specific research, not a generic answer
- "Transformers and diffusion models" — signals GenAI literacy relevant to the JD
- "Financial inclusion / credit access" — shows understanding of Experian's domain impact
- "From day one" — signals confidence and readiness

## Q2 — Teamwork to Achieve a Goal

**Full question:**  
*"At Experian, we believe in the power of collaboration. Tell us about a time when you worked as part of a team to achieve a goal."*

**Competency tested:** Collaboration, communication, shared ownership, interpersonal effectiveness

---

### 📝 Polished Answer (~90 seconds)

During my second year of graduate research, my lab undertook a collaborative project that required input from three different research areas: signal processing, machine learning, and domain science. I was the ML lead on a team of four.

The goal was to build a predictive model that combined physical domain knowledge with learned representations from data — a genuinely cross-disciplinary challenge. The difficulty was that each team member used different tooling, had different terminology, and had different mental models of what "good results" looked like.

My specific contribution was to serve as the technical bridge. I met individually with the domain scientist to understand what features were physically meaningful — information that pure ML approaches would miss. I then translated those insights into engineered features that I incorporated into our pipeline. I also established a shared evaluation protocol so that everyone understood how we were measuring success and could contribute to interpreting results.

When our initial results were below expectations, I organized a structured review session where each team member walked through their component. We identified that the signal preprocessing step wasn't applying the correct normalization for the ML model's assumptions — a cross-domain issue that none of us would have caught individually.

The corrected pipeline achieved significantly stronger results, and the collaboration process — where domain knowledge, engineering rigor, and ML methodology all contributed — became a model for how we structured subsequent team projects. The experience reinforced for me that the best ML teams aren't made of ML people alone — they're made of communicators.

---

### 🔑 Key Phrases to Include

- "Technical bridge" — leadership within collaboration, not just participation
- "Individually met with" — shows proactive communication
- "Shared evaluation protocol" — concrete process contribution
- "The best ML teams are made of communicators" — memorable closing insight

## Q3 — Learning Something New

**Full question:**  
*"At Experian, we are always learning and improving. Tell us about a time you had to learn something new quickly and effectively."*

**Competency tested:** Learning agility, intellectual curiosity, self-directed growth

---

### 📝 Polished Answer (~88 seconds)

About a year into my Master's research, it became clear that my thesis work would benefit significantly from transformer-based sequence models — but my background at that point was primarily in classical signal processing and convolutional neural networks. I had very limited hands-on experience with attention mechanisms or the Hugging Face ecosystem.

I had approximately four weeks before a research milestone where I needed to demonstrate whether a transformer approach was viable for my problem. So I structured a rapid self-learning plan.

In the first week, I focused on building conceptual understanding: I worked through the "Attention Is All You Need" paper carefully, read Jay Alammar's illustrated transformer blog posts, and watched Andrej Karpathy's lecture on building transformers from scratch. In week two, I moved to hands-on implementation — fine-tuning a small BERT-based model on a toy version of my dataset using Hugging Face Transformers, just to understand the API end to end. Weeks three and four I spent adapting the approach to my actual research problem, debugging positional encoding issues and tuning training hyperparameters.

By the milestone, I had a working transformer-based baseline that I could compare against my existing architecture. The transformer approach didn't outperform my hybrid model on my specific problem, but I now had a rigorous justification for my architectural choices — and I had developed a reusable familiarity with the Hugging Face ecosystem that I've used in every project since.

The lesson I took away: effective fast learning isn't about consuming everything — it's about structured progression from understanding to doing as quickly as possible.

---

### 🔑 Key Phrases to Include

- Named specific resources (Alammar blog, Karpathy lecture, original paper) — shows authentic learning
- "Week 1 / week 2" structure — demonstrates organized approach, not chaotic cramming
- "Didn't outperform, but now had rigorous justification" — intellectual honesty builds credibility
- "Reusable familiarity" — shows transfer of learning across projects

## Q4 — Creative Problem Solving

**Full question:**  
*"At Experian, we welcome innovation. Tell us about a time you found a creative or innovative way to solve a problem."*

**Competency tested:** Innovation, creative thinking, problem-solving agility, ownership

---

### 📝 Polished Answer (~90 seconds)

In a key phase of my thesis research, I hit a wall that many supervised learning researchers face: I had a promising model architecture but a critically limited labeled dataset. Standard data augmentation techniques existed, but they were designed for image or text data — not for the time-series signal domain I was working in.

Rather than abandoning the direction or waiting to collect more data, I proposed a hybrid solution that combined two ideas I hadn't seen combined in this context before.

First, I adapted frequency-domain augmentation from the signal processing literature — applying controlled perturbations in the frequency spectrum rather than in the raw time domain — to generate synthetic training samples that preserved the physically meaningful properties of the signals. Second, I used a simple conditional variational autoencoder to learn a generative model of the data distribution and sample additional synthetic examples from it.

Neither approach was completely novel in isolation. The creative contribution was combining them in a systematic pipeline: using the physics-informed augmentation to bootstrap the VAE training, then using the VAE to generate a broader distribution of samples, and finally validating that the synthetic data improved held-out test performance rather than just inflating training set size artificially.

The augmented pipeline improved model F1 by about 7 percentage points and allowed the research to proceed without requiring additional labeled data collection, which would have taken months.

What I learned from that experience is that innovation in engineering often isn't about inventing entirely new ideas — it's about seeing where existing ideas from adjacent fields can be productively combined to solve a problem in front of you.

---

### 🔑 Key Phrases to Include

- "Physics-informed augmentation" — technical specificity builds credibility
- "Hadn't seen combined in this context" — honest framing of the innovation
- "Validating that synthetic data improved held-out test performance" — shows rigorous evaluation mindset
- Final insight about combining adjacent ideas — memorable, quotable closing

## Delivery & Pacing Notes

| Dimension | Target | Watch Out For |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| **Pace** | ~130–150 words per minute | Speaking too fast when nervous |
| **Eye contact** | Camera lens, not your image on screen | Looking down at notes |
| **Sentence length** | Short to medium — vary for rhythm | Long run-on sentences lose clarity |
| **Opening** | Set the scene within 10 seconds | Starting with "Um" or "So" |
| **Closing** | Always end on Result or Reflection | Trailing off or saying "yeah, so..." |
| **Filler words** | Minimize "like," "um," "basically" | These reduce perceived confidence |
| **Energy** | Engaged, not flat — slight forward lean | Monotone delivery reads as disinterest |

### 90-Second Word Count Reference

> At ~140 words/minute, **90 seconds = approximately 210 words**.  
> A tight STAR answer is typically 190–230 words.  
> Practice until your answers land between 75–90 seconds naturally.
